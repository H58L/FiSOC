Hyperinflation can take your entire life's savings, without the government having to bother raising taxes. -Thomas Sowell

Have you ever wondered why countries can't just print more money to get out of debt, or to fix unemployment?
Theoretically, they could. Unfortunately, the consequences could be catastrophic.
For instance:
Back in the 1990s in Zimbabwe, President Robert Mugabe printed large amounts of money.
This extra money entered the financial ecosystem and lead to an increase in prices. Goods and services became less affordable. People hoarded essential items because they would be too expensive the next day.
In 2008 The African nation’s inflation rate in November rose by a whopping 79,600,000,000%! 1 US$ worth as much as 50 million Zimbabwean dollars!
An analogous situation occurred in the Weimar Republic (Germany) in the 1920s. At a point, it cost more to print a note than the note was worth.

So what exactly is this predicament?
Inflation - A general increase in the prices of goods and services in an economy.
In 1994, a litre of milk cost 8.50₹. However, 28 years later, in 2022, 1-litre milk is 55₹. That means inflation gradually reduced the purchasing power of money.
It's a classic game of demand and supply.

Let’s take another example:
Assuming you have a book, priced at 100 Rs, but there are 100 buyers willing to buy the book at the said rate. What will you do in that case?
You will simply increase the price of the book until one buyer agrees to purchase the book at the quoted price. This is the principle of demand and supply and it’s exactly what drives inflation.

If the value of a currency goes down, imports become expensive and so economic activity decreases.
What can the government do instead of printing more money?


Contractionary monetary policy is now a more popular way method of controlling inflation. The government increases the interest rates to reduce the money supply within the economy.

Basically, Increasing prices —> Government increases interest rates
decreasing prices —> Government decreases interest rates

Interest rates are increased, encouraging people to put money in banks thus taking it out of circulation.
While interest rates levied on loans are escalated, disincentivising borrowing money from the banks hence reducing the amount of money circulating in the market.
A decline in the amount of money flowing in the market leads to a dip in prices.
