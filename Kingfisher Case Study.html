"Rather than spending millions getting film stars, I am quite happy to be the brand ambassador myself."      -Vijay Mallya

 

Born as the heir of a liquor empire, Vijay Mallya – dubbed the “King of Good Times”, assumed the role of the Chairman of United Breweries at the age of 28. Mallya had the vision to grow the business and hence ventured into numerous different industries ranging from the alcohol beverage industry to the fertiliser industry. The Kingfisher beer by United Breweries evolved into a “lifestyle” which preached extravagance.

2005

Mallya set foot in the aviation industry aiming to recreate a luxurious flying experience and Kingfisher Airlines took its maiden flight from Mumbai to Delhi in 2005. It attracted almost 1/4th of domestic travellers and soon became the 2nd largest airline in the domestic market.

Despite this, a series of poor decisions led to the downfall of Kingfisher airlines and consequently that of Vijay Mallya.


2007

Mallya wanted to expand Kingfisher airlines into international travel and compete with the global market. However, according to Indian law, he couldn’t do so unless Kingfisher had been functional for at least 5 years. Thus, he purchased Air Deccan and rebranded it as Kingfisher red as a low-cost alternative to the luxurious Kingfisher.   A constantly changing business model brought down the brand image and confused the consumers.


2008

The global recession hit the aviation industry hard and Kingfisher had to increase ticket prices which lost them customers.


2011

Kingfisher declared that it had cash flow issues since the operational costs were higher than profits. The Airport Authority of India placed the airline on a ‘cash and carry’ basis which meant that they had to pay upfront in cash for daily purchases of products and services as opposed to paying the airports at the end of every month.

To keep the airline up and running, Mallya took loans from 17 banks amounting to Rs7000 crores, most of which were from public sector banks. Multiple controversies exist with regard to the acquisition of these loans as they were granted when kingfisher was already bleeding money. Current assets and the goodwill of Kingfisher were placed as collateral to acquire the loans, which created suspicion about the involvement of bank officials in the Mallya scam.

Furthermore, he diverted these funds through multiple banks to fund his IPL team – Royal Challengers Bangalore and his Formula One team – Force India. Kingfisher airlines was accused of acting as a vehicle to launder approximately Rs 9990 crore.


2012

All of this was done at the expense of delayed payments to vendors and stakeholders. Employees were not paid for almost 15 months. during which they started multiple strikes and quit their jobs. On 20th December 2012 Kingfisher’s flying licence was ultimately suspended.


2016

In March 2016, the loan became an outstanding Rs 9091 crore. Mallya then agreed to pay the principal amount of Rs7000 crore on the condition that his other loans be waived. However, banks didn’t agree to this; therefore, Mallya fled to Britain where he now resides as an economic fugitive. He is also fighting his extradition to India.


Now

The Vijay Mallya case is a glaring magnifying lens into the loopholes in our banking system that enable such misdoings and loss of taxpayer money. The business tycoon was labelled as the first fugitive economic offender of the nation and is now remembered for the same.

Other economic fugitives include Nirav Modi, Mehul Choksi, Chetankumar Sandesera, Jatin Mehta, Lalit Modi, etc.

About 72 Indians charged with financial irregularities currently live abroad.
